Learn

Plain-language guides on common mental-health topics — what they can look like, that help exists, and when to reach out.

This is general education, not a diagnosis or a substitute for an assessment by a professional. If something here sounds familiar and it's affecting your daily life, that's a good reason to reach out — start with Find Care.

Common topics

Anxiety

Anxiety is more than ordinary worry. It can show up as worrying excessively about everyday things, trouble controlling the worry, feeling restless or "on edge," difficulty concentrating or sleeping, and physical signs like muscle tension or an upset stomach. Anxiety disorders are very common and very treatable — often with therapy, medication, or both. If anxiety starts to interfere with your work, school, or relationships, that's a good time to reach out.

Depression

Depression is more than a passing low mood. It can look like a persistent sad, anxious, or "empty" feeling, loss of interest in things you used to enjoy, fatigue, trouble sleeping (or sleeping too much), changes in appetite, and difficulty concentrating. The good news: depression is treatable, and most people improve with therapy, medication, or both. If these feelings last most of the day, nearly every day, for two weeks or more, talk with a provider. (If you have thoughts of suicide, call or text 988.)

Grief & loss

Grief is a natural response to loss, and it can bring a wide range of emotions — sadness, anger, even exhaustion — along with physical effects. All of these are normal. There's no "right" way to grieve and no fixed timeline, and your experience may look different from someone else's. Support from people you trust helps. If grief gets more intense over time instead of gradually easing, or it keeps you from functioning, it's worth reaching out to a grief counselor.

Stress & burnout

Short bursts of stress are a normal part of life, but long-term stress can take a real toll on body and mind. Burnout is a kind of work-related stress — feeling worn out, becoming cynical or detached from your job, and feeling less effective than you used to. If stress feels constant or overwhelming, or it's affecting your sleep, health, or relationships, that's a signal to reach out for support.

Sleep

Trouble falling asleep, waking through the night, or feeling tired after a full night's sleep are all common — and they often go hand in hand with mental health, each making the other worse. Many sleep problems improve with steadier habits (see While You Wait). But if poor sleep is persistent and draining your days, it's worth talking with a provider.

Trauma

Trauma is an emotional response to a deeply distressing or frightening event (or events). Reactions vary widely and can include flashbacks, trouble sleeping, feeling on guard, or avoiding reminders — and they can show up right away or much later. These responses are understandable, and effective treatments exist. If trauma is affecting your daily life, a mental-health professional can help; see therapy approaches below.

Understanding therapy: common approaches

There are many kinds of therapy, and most have solid research behind them. You don't need to memorize the names — fit with your therapist matters more than the acronym.

CBT — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

Helps you notice the thoughts and behaviors that keep you stuck and practice more helpful ones. Structured, practical, present-focused. Strong evidence for depression, anxiety, and more.

DBT — Dialectical Behavior Therapy

Teaches concrete skills for handling intense emotions, easing distress in the moment, and improving relationships. Blends acceptance with change, and often uses mindfulness.

ACT — Acceptance & Commitment Therapy

Helps you make room for hard thoughts and feelings instead of fighting them, and take action based on what matters to you. Builds "psychological flexibility."

EMDR

A structured therapy for trauma: you briefly focus on a distressing memory while the therapist guides a back-and-forth movement, helping your brain reprocess it so it feels less raw. Well-established for PTSD.

Psychodynamic / talk therapy

Helps you understand yourself more deeply — patterns from your past or relationships that shape how you feel today — through open, nonjudgmental conversation that builds insight.

The bottom line
