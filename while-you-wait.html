While you wait for care

You've decided to reach out — that's a real step, and the hardest one. Getting in to see a therapist can take time. The ideas below are simple things many people find steadying in the meantime.

These are coping support, not treatment, and they don't replace care from a professional. Try one or two that appeal to you — there's no wrong place to start. If things feel like too much, you don't have to wait: call or text 988 any time.

Right now: settle your body

Paced breathing. When you're anxious, slowing your breath helps calm your body's stress response. Breathe in slowly through your nose for a count of about 4, gently hold for 4, and breathe out slowly for 4 — letting your belly rise as you inhale. Repeat for a few rounds, or a few minutes.

Grounding: 5-4-3-2-1. When your mind is racing, come back to the present using your senses. Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, and 1 you can taste. Go slowly, and pair it with a few slow breaths. It's a small reset you can do anywhere.

Today: the basics that protect your mental health

Small, steady changes add up — you don't have to do all of these.

Sleep

Try to sleep and wake at the same times, keep the room dark, quiet, and cool, and put screens away ~30 minutes before bed. Go easy on afternoon caffeine and late meals.

Move a little

Movement is one of the most reliable mood-lifters there is — and some benefit starts right after you move. No workout needed; a short walk counts. Outside is even better.

Reach out to one person

You don't need a big circle — one trusted person is enough. A short "rough day here" keeps you from carrying it alone; you don't need the right words.

Keep a little structure

A simple daily rhythm — regular meals, steady sleep, one or two small plans — gives you something to hold onto. Keep it gentle, not rigid.

Go easy on alcohol

It's common to reach for a drink to take the edge off, but alcohol can worsen anxiety and low mood and disrupt sleep. Cutting back is one concrete thing that helps.

Take breaks from the scroll

A steady stream of distressing news ("doomscrolling") can genuinely lower your mood. Turn off notifications, keep check-ins short, and keep your phone away from meals and bed.

Write it down

Putting thoughts into words frees up mental space. Try writing for 15–20 minutes about what's weighing on you, a few days in a row. No one else has to read it.

Knowing what to expect can take the edge off. Learn how a first therapy session usually goes, how to find the right fit, and questions you might bring — and remember it's okay to switch if it's not a match.

What to expect & how to find care →

If thoughts of self-harm come up: please reach out now — you deserve support and don't have to face this alone. Call or text 988 any time (free, confidential). If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 911. It can also help to make a simple safety plan — a short list of your warning signs, things that calm you, and people to contact — which you can start today or build with a counselor.
